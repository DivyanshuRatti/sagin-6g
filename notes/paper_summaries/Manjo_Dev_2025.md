## Paper Summary

**Full Citation:** K. Dev, S. A. Khowaja, K. Singh, E. Zeydan, and M. Debbah, "Advanced Architectures Integrated with Agentic AI for Next-Generation Wireless Networks," arXiv preprint arXiv:2502.01089, 2025. https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.01089

## Summary (3-5 sentences in your own words)

The paper explores how Agentic AI can be integrated into future 6G wireless networks to make them more intelligent, efficient, and autonomous. Instead of relying on human operators to manage every network task, the authors propose AI agents that can monitor network conditions, make decisions, and optimize performance in real time. The paper also discusses new architectures such as simplified control and user planes, serverless computing, constrained AI, and neural radio protocol stacks to improve scalability, reduce operational costs, and support future applications like connected vehicles (V2X). Finally, the authors identify several open challenges, including security, interoperability, and the computational demands of deploying AI at scale.

## Relevance to Our Survey Topic

This paper is highly relevant to Space-Air-Ground Integrated Networks (SAGIN) because these networks connect satellites, aircraft, drones, and terrestrial infrastructure, creating a highly dynamic environment that is difficult to manage manually. Agentic AI could continuously monitor network conditions across all layers and automatically allocate resources, optimize routing, and adapt to changing traffic or link conditions. These capabilities would improve the reliability, efficiency, and scalability of future SAGIN and 6G communication systems.

## Critical Comment

I think the paper's experiment has one limitation because it only tested a V2X (vehicle-to-everything) environment with cars on the road. While this shows that the proposed architecture can work in a ground-based network, it does not prove that it will perform the same way in a SAGIN environment. In a SAGIN network, satellites, drones, and ground stations operate at different altitudes, move at different speeds, and experience different communication delays. These factors make the network much more complex than a road-based V2X system. I think the authors should also test their architecture in a realistic SAGIN scenario before concluding that it can effectively support next-generation integrated wireless networks.
