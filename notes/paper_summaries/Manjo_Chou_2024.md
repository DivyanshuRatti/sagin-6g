# Paper Summary

**Full Citation:** Chou, H., Solanki, S., Ha, V. N., Chen, L., Ma, S. L., Al-Hraishawi, H., Eappen, G., Querol, J., Merlano-Duncan, J. C., & Chatzinotas, S. (2024). Edge AI Empowered Physical Layer Security for 6G NTN: Potential Threats and Future Opportunities. arXiv:2401.01005.

## Summary (3–5 sentences in your own words)

This paper discusses how Edge AI can improve the security of 6G Non-Terrestrial Networks (NTNs), which include satellites, drones, and high-altitude platforms. The authors explain that future wireless networks will face new security threats because they will connect many different devices across space, air, and ground. The paper explores how artificial intelligence can detect attacks, protect communications, and improve physical layer security by making faster decisions close to where the data is generated, using techniques like federated learning and split deep learning to keep that processing close to the edge. It also identifies current challenges, including limited computing resources, privacy concerns, and the need for more reliable AI models in real-world 6G networks.

## Relevance to Our Survey

This paper is highly relevant to Space-Air-Ground Integrated Networks (SAGIN) because SAGIN relies on communication between satellites, aerial platforms, and ground stations. These networks are more vulnerable to interference, spoofing, jamming, and eavesdropping due to their wide coverage and dynamic environment. Using Edge AI allows security decisions to be made closer to the network edge, helping detect threats more quickly while reducing communication delays. This can improve both the security and performance of future SAGIN deployments.

## Critical Comment

One limitation of the paper is that it mainly discusses future possibilities instead of providing extensive real-world testing. Although the proposed Edge AI techniques appear promising, more experiments in realistic 6G NTN or SAGIN environments are needed to show how well they perform under different conditions. Since satellites, drones, and ground stations have different communication delays and hardware limitations, additional testing would make the proposed solutions more convincing. Overall, the paper provides useful ideas but would benefit from stronger experimental validation.
